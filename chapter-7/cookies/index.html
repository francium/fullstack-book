Early Networks

The 1960's and 1970's laid the foundation of modern networking, computer network and the future invention of the internet. During the 1960's many different groups were interested in computer networks and had begun, at roughly the same time, doing research and development into early computer networks. By the 1970's initial computer applications had begun to come out that utilized the early computer networks. The most notable being electronic mail (email), which became one of the most widely used networked applications and foreshadowed the boom of "people-to-people" traffic that would take place on the modern internet a few decades in the future.

For the first few decades, from the 1960s to the 1980s, the only places that had access to the network, the ARPANET, were universities, colleges, government organization and military organizations. Over time, the network expanded to other countries and by the 1990s the original ARPANET had grown into the internet that spanned the entire globe.

By the 1980s personal computers were beginning to be widely available and had started to become common in everyday life. During this period the internet spread from it's original educational and research roots into a general purpose utility for both personal and commercial use. Then open nature of the internet nurtured and encouraged its uptake and growth. The underlying infrastructure, the protocols that facilitate data transfer on the internet, provided a general purpose platform on top of which a wide variety of applications could thrive.

In 1989 Tim Berners-Lee purposed a hypertext system and pioneered (citation needed) the early world wide web. The 1990s were a frenzy of activity. The NSF lifted restrictions on commercial use of the internet and the flood gates really opened for the modern internet.

Of course a thorough accounting of the history and events surrounding the early networks and internet could take up multiple books alone. Interested readers can find starting points in the references at the end of this chapter.

The Internet and the World Wide Web

The 1990's and 2000's were a frenzy of activity. Billions in investment were being made in commercial sector. This frenzy led to many fundamental building blocks, innovations and key components of the internet, but it resulted in a massive economic bubble at the turn of the century, now known as the Dotcom Bubble. Many companies, without sound fundamentals, came and went during the period. But others, now common names that almost anyone with a electronic device and access to the internet would know, came out of this period and went on to become some of the largest and most powerful companies to exist.

The first three decades of the internet, 1990's, 2000's and 2010's came be split into two periods: the Web 1.0 era and the Web 2.0 era. The Web 1.0 era saw websites that were primarily static and without many ways for user to engage with. This all changed in the 2000's when websites became dynamic and emphasized user-generated content.

The 2010's saw the rise of internet giants, whose platforms, no longer just mere websites, became integrated into daily life of billions of users. Yet, while a handful of giants became the face of the modern internet, countless others build their businesses on the internet. It is said every company is now a technology company -- at least in one way of another, as very few companies can now afford to or practically functional without utilizing the internet.

Similarly, modern life is now so reliant on the internet that if the internet stopped working modern life itself would come to a standstill. Without much effort you can see how much of your life relies on the internet: phones, video games, technology in classrooms, online banking, credit cards, socialization (social networks), television/movies, commerce/shopping, office work, logistics, navigation, government services, hospitals, news and numerous other examples.

The Future

It's hard to predict the future, but one thing is for sure: the internet will continue to exist and be a big part of daily life. The interesting thing about the internet is that is is constantly evolving. It's a dynamic network that changes over time. While the core principles will continue to exist, the internet in 2030 or 2040 and beyond may look and be used quite differently than it was in the 1990's or 2000's or the 2010's.

A few ideas worth mentoring are: decentralization, centralization, commercialization, balkanization and Web 3.0.

Let's look at decentralization and centralization. It may sound contradictory, but there's an ongoing tension between forces that are pulling the internet towards decentralization and centralization. If you look at the very nature of the internet, it was based around the idea of anyone being able to host a server and anyone else on the same network (the internet at the global level) being able to access it. This allowed a natural decentral architecture of the internet to develop. There are numerous other protocol that encourage this such as BitTorrent and more recently the Fediverse. In contrast, during the 2000s and more so during the 2010s we saw a great wave of centralization of the internet due to large commercial internet companies dominating key internet markets. This has led to isolated communities that are highly controlled and in a sense isolated from the rest of the internet. One viewpoint of the future may envision a future of even more centralization and a slow decline of decentralized nature of the internet. Another may envision of a future that has the internet go back to its decentralized roots and embrace decentralization.

The discussion of centralization also leads to a discussion of the commercialization of the internet. Since the 1990's the internet has become the primary means of conducting business. More recently companies have begun to operate on the notion of selling attention rather than selling products. Regardless, companies are increasingly looking to commercialize the internet for financial gain. This is a far cry from the original research and educational roots of the internet. In the future it's likely to continue as we see companies continues to build businesses on the internet.

Balkanization is the idea the internet could become fragments into isolated networks with little exchange and only across tightly controlled exchange points. There are numerous examples of governments of various that have implemented restrictions on access to the internet for their citizens. This is not a book about geopolitics so we will refrain for mentioning specifics. However, it's possible due to geopolitical circumstances we see a rise in restrictions and self-imposed isolation of the internet between countries.

Fullstack Development

We will close off this chapter with a high level overview of fullstack development. A fullstack application, in the context of the internet and computer networks, generally refers to a system of networked computer programs that are engineered to work together to allow a user to perform some task. Many fullstack applications consist of 3 major components: a user interface, an application server, and a data storage layer. There's no hard rule and additional components are integrated as necessary for the application to function. In contrast, some people refer to fullstack in a more general sense: systems that also include hardware design, firmwares, operating systems, other low level components, or any combination of these. We will use the term fullstack in a more narrow scope: internet and networked systems that consist of a user interface, an application server, data storage layer and possibly other higher level components, but without including any lower level components in our design and engineering efforts.

The design and architecture of a fullstack application can vary greatly. It really depends on the functional and non-functional requirements of the problem being solved. For example, a home security system may closely follow the 3 component design we previously mentioned: a user interface for the user to interact with the system, an application server that will handle requests from the user interface and handle incoming data feeds (security cameras, sensors, etc), and a data storage layer for storing data about the security system and data feeds. On the other hand, at the very other extreme, for example, an online shopping website may have multiple user interfaces, multiple application servers, and multiple data storage layers, as well as other components and integrations. In simple cases, the entire system may be running on a single computer or server, but as the system gets more complex it may be split up and run on multiple distinct servers. There a generally three options system designers can choose when deploying a fullstack application: the end user's computer, servers your own and manages yourself, servers that someone else owns and manages for you or an combination of these options.

Servers and Computers

Throughout this book you may see the words "server" and "computer". A server is a computer, but a computer may or may not be a server. A server is a computer used to serve data for other computers and handle requetss from other computers.

Generally your own computer wouldn't be a server. But if you're developing fullstack applications then it may be considered a server temporary while you are running an application server on it.

An application server is a computer program that utilizes network protocols and the underlying networking capabilities of a computer to serve data and handle requests.
