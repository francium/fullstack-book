Chapter 2 Networks and Protocols

The simplest possible definition of a network could be: a collection of interconnected nodes. A protocol is the agreement between nodes or applications on how to send messages to each other. How the nodes are nodes and how they communicate amongst each other is the focus of this chapter.

Networks

Network Architecture an Topology

There's an exponential number of ways in which you could connect a group of nodes together, but having nodes organized and connected in arbitrary ways may not be ideal in all cases. Some use cases like peer-to-peer systems, direct connections between and all computers in a network is a desirable trait. However, in a large business, having every node connected to every other can be a security and maintenance disaster. Choosing how your computers are organized in a network depends largely on the requirements of the systems you're designing.

Some of the most common network architectures, or topologies, are[1]:

  • Ring
  • Mesh
  • Start
  • Line
  • Tree
  • Bus
  • Fully Connected

Looking at the real world we can discuss common network architectures being used in practice as well. At the highest level there is the internet, which can be best described as a "network of networks"[2]. Before we describe the architecture of the internet, let's look at smaller networks. Most users would gain access to the internet through an internet service provider (ISP). An ISP have may be one or more intermediary nodes between your local network and the rest of the internet. Your local network would likely follow the star topology in the simplest case. Revisiting the internet, we can describe the overall structure of the internet as a recursive structure where at the highest level you have large internet infrastructure providers who connect smaller internet infrastructure providers together. One level below you have smaller providers and so on. Hence "network of networks".
